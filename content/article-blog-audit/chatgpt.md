I would audit it in layers, not as one blurry “is this good” check.

A strong article can still fail because the wrong layer is broken. Sometimes the writing is good but the page is structurally mute. Sometimes the page is clean but the facts are soft. Sometimes the facts are fine but the article does not deserve retrieval for any real query. So I split the audit into seven passes.

First pass is intent and target query fit.

Before touching the article, I define what job it is supposed to do. Is it trying to rank for a search query, get cited by AI systems, convert readers into signups, support a product claim, or build brand authority? Then I map the likely search intents behind it: informational, comparative, commercial investigation, navigational, or problem-solving. If the article is trying to do all five at once, that is already a red flag.

I ask:
what exact questions should this article answer better than competitors
what kind of reader is it for
what would make this page the best citation candidate for that query
what action should happen after reading

If those answers are fuzzy, the article is usually overbroad.

Second pass is content quality and relevance.

Here I ignore metadata and look at the body like a ruthless editor.

I check whether the article opens with a clear problem, whether it answers the promised question early, whether the headings reflect real sub-questions, and whether each section earns its place. I look for fluff, repeated claims, vague language, filler transitions, and paragraphs that sound smart but carry no usable information.

I score things like:
clarity of thesis
specificity
original insight
evidence density
usefulness per paragraph
reader payoff

I also test whether the article is actually query-aligned. If someone searched the likely keyword, would this page satisfy them fast, or would they bounce because it took too long to get concrete.

Third pass is factual integrity.

This is where a lot of “good SEO content” quietly dies.

I isolate every factual claim, statistic, named platform, quoted behavior, product feature, timeline, technical assertion, legal implication, and competitive statement. Then I classify each one:

verified and sourced
likely true but unsupported
outdated risk
self-reported product claim
speculative inference
dangerous overstatement

This matters because AI systems and human readers both punish sloppiness, just in different ways. Humans lose trust. Retrieval systems get weaker evidence. If an article says “nobody else does this” or “all AI models behave like X,” that needs proof or softer phrasing.

For product-led articles, I also compare every claim in the article against what the product actually does. A page that promises more than the software can show is not just a copy problem. It is a trust leak.

Fourth pass is structure and extractability.

This is the pass most people skip, and it matters for both SEO and AEO.

I check whether the article can be easily parsed by both humans and machines. That means:
one clean H1
logical H2 and H3 hierarchy
short answer blocks near the top
clear definitions
lists where lists help
comparison tables where comparison matters
quotes or callouts only when they add signal
tight paragraphing
minimal ambiguity in pronouns and references

Then I test extractability:
if an AI system grabbed only one chunk, would that chunk still make sense
does each section answer a distinct sub-question
are there direct declarative sentences that can stand alone as citations
are key claims surrounded by supporting context, not buried in metaphor

A lot of articles are beautiful to read but terrible to extract from. If every paragraph depends on the previous one, answer engines have less to grab cleanly.

Fifth pass is SEO surface area.

Now I move to classic search mechanics.

I review title tag logic, meta description, slug, internal linking, image alt text, canonical handling, schema opportunities, entity naming consistency, and whether the page has enough semantic support to rank for its topic. I also check whether the target term is overused, underused, or used in a way that feels forced.

I want:
title and H1 aligned but not identical clones
intro that reinforces topic naturally
headings that mirror real search phrasing
internal links to supporting and commercial pages
outbound citations where factual trust matters
clean entity repetition without keyword stuffing

I also check whether the article creates its own long-tail surface. A strong piece does not just target one head keyword. It answers clusters of adjacent questions.

Sixth pass is AEO and citation readiness.

This is where I stop thinking like a search crawler and start thinking like an answer engine.

I look for:
direct answers to likely prompts
named entities stated clearly
definitions written in plain language
factual claims tied to evidence
sections that can be quoted cleanly
strong co-occurrence between the topic, brand, and supporting entities
visible trust signals like author identity, publication date, update date, product evidence, source references, and real examples

Then I test what I call chunk worthiness:
is this article made of citation-worthy blocks
does it contain sentences a model would feel safe repeating
does it show enough evidence to survive summarization without becoming mush
is it original enough to deserve retrieval over ten similar pages

AEO is not magic. It is structured clarity plus evidence plus extractable usefulness.

Seventh pass is business relevance.

A technically good article can still be a dead asset if it does not connect to the business.

So I ask:
does this article bring the reader toward the product, service, email list, or authority goal
does it create a next step that fits the reader’s state of awareness
is the CTA too early, too late, too weak, or too thirsty
does the article naturally bridge from pain to solution without turning into a sales brochure

If an article gets traffic but never moves belief, it is a pretty ceiling with no stairs.

My actual scoring model would look something like this:

Content quality 25
Fact integrity 20
Structure and extractability 15
SEO fundamentals 15
AEO and citation readiness 15
Business alignment 10

Then I leave notes in three buckets:
what is broken
what is missing
what is strong enough to amplify

The way I would physically perform the audit is simple.

I would first read it cold like a normal user.
Then I would read it again with markup, tagging every claim, heading, weak paragraph, query mismatch, and citation opportunity.
Then I would compare it against the top-ranking and top-cited pages for the same topic.
Then I would produce:
a scorecard
a line-by-line issue list
rewrite priorities
quick wins
and a final verdict on whether this piece should be refreshed, expanded, merged, or killed

If you want the sharp practical version, here is the core test:

Does it answer a real question clearly
Does it say anything specific
Can its claims be trusted
Can its sections be extracted cleanly
Does it deserve retrieval
Does it move the right reader somewhere useful

If even one of those fails badly, the article is leaking value.

Use this as a real scoring rubric, not a vague checklist. Score each section from 0 to 5, then multiply by the weight. The final score lands out of 100.

A 5 means the article is strong enough to compete and be cited.
A 3 means usable but leaking value.
A 1 means weak, risky, or unclear.
A 0 means missing or actively harmful.

    0: Brand/topic relationship is muddy

    0: No author/date/source/context

    0: Wrong audience

    0: No bridge

    3: Basic connection
    5: Natural movement from pain to solution without sounding desperate

    0: No next step

    3: Functional CTA
    5: Clear next step matched to reader intent and article stage

28. Strategic value to the business
    0: Traffic bait only
    1: Little brand or conversion value
    3: Moderate value
    5: Strong authority, relevance, and conversion support

Scoring method

Score each item 0 to 5.

Then calculate:

(item score ÷ 5) × item category weight share

To keep it simple, use category averages:

Content average × 25
Fact average × 20
Structure average × 15
SEO average × 15
AEO average × 15
Business average × 10

Example:
If Content averages 4.0, that contributes 20 points.
If Facts averages 3.0, that contributes 12 points.

Final score bands

90–100: Retrieval-ready, high trust, strong candidate for ranking and citation
80–89: Strong piece, minor leaks
70–79: Good foundation, several weak points
60–69: Usable but underperforming
40–59: Major problems, rewrite recommended
0–39: Broken, misleading, or strategically weak

Use these verdict labels at the end

Keep
The article is strong enough to preserve with minor edits

Refresh
The article has value but needs updates, proof, structure, or targeting fixes

Rebuild
The idea is good but the article should be substantially rewritten

Merge
The article is too thin or overlapping and should be combined with another piece

Kill
The piece is off-strategy, weak, misleading, or not worth saving

13. Citation Discovery and Authority Monitoring

This section covers the layer most auditors ignore entirely. You can write a great article, optimize it for search and AI systems, and still have no idea whether it is actually being mentioned, cited, or discussed anywhere. If you are serious about citation readiness and entity authority, you need an ongoing discovery process that does not require paid tools or API keys.

Search operator mention sweeps

Run these regularly against Google and Bing. Replace the placeholders with your actual brand, domain, founder, and product names.

General web mentions:
"YourBrand" -site:yourdomain.com
"YourBrand" OR "Your Brand" -site:yourdomain.com
"YourBrand" "review" -site:yourdomain.com
"yourdomain.com" -site:yourdomain.com

Platform-specific:
"YourBrand" site:reddit.com
"yourdomain.com" site:reddit.com
"YourBrand" site:x.com
"YourBrand" site:youtube.com
"YourBrand" site:trustpilot.com
"YourBrand" site:reddit.com OR site:stackexchange.com OR site:quora.com

Competitor comparison:
"YourBrand" OR "CompetitorOne" OR "CompetitorTwo" -site:yourdomain.com

Founder and team:
"YourFounder" "YourBrand"
"YourFounder" "yourdomain.com"

Negative sentiment early warning:
"YourBrand" "scam" OR "fraud" -site:yourdomain.com

Save these as browser bookmarks and rerun at least weekly.

Free alert systems (no API keys)

Google Alerts — set up alerts for your brand name, domain, founder name, product names, and key competitors. Covers news, blogs, and general web. Unlimited alerts, delivered by email.

Talkwalker Alerts — functions like Google Alerts but uses a different index. Good as a second opinion or backup layer.

F5Bot — free email alerts for keyword mentions on Reddit, Hacker News, and Lobsters. Extremely useful for catching discussions you would otherwise miss entirely.

ForumScout — free tier with 3 keyword alerts and roughly 100 mentions per month across forums, social, blogs, and news. Updates every 6 hours. 14-day retention on the free plan.

None of these require API keys. They just need an email address.

Manual native platform searches

Some platforms resist scraping but have decent built-in search. Use these directly:

Twitter/X: search "YourBrand" and your main hashtags. Save as pinned searches or TweetDeck columns.
Reddit: search your brand name and sort by "new" to catch fresh threads before they age out.
Facebook/Instagram: search brand name and hashtags, then follow or save relevant results.
YouTube: search your brand name and domain. Check if anyone is reviewing, mentioning, or referencing your content in video.

Entity clarity verification

Every time you audit an article, confirm:
is the primary entity (brand, product, person) named clearly in the first paragraph
does the article define what it is, who it serves, and why it matters within the first 150 words
is the entity name used consistently (no mid-article switches between abbreviations, nicknames, or variations)
do supporting entities (competitors, platforms, tools) appear with their full names at first mention
is there strong co-occurrence between the brand name and the topic it wants to own

If an AI model reads this article, it should leave with a clear, unambiguous understanding of who the entity is and what they do. Muddy entity naming kills citation potential.

Authority signal audit

Check whether the article and its surrounding page carry real authority signals:
named author with a bio
publication date and last-updated date
outbound citations to credible sources
inbound links from relevant domains (check with a free backlink tool like Ahrefs Webmaster Tools)
mentions in third-party content (use the search operators above to find these)
reviews, testimonials, or independent references

If authority signals are thin, the article may rank but will rarely be cited by AI systems or treated as a primary source.

Mention logging workflow

To make this systematic instead of random, use a simple tracking sheet with these columns:

date_found, source_type, platform, query_used, author_or_site, title_or_thread, content_snippet, url, sentiment, priority, action_needed, owner, status, notes

Run the workflow weekly:
1. Rerun saved Google/Bing search operators
2. Check alert emails from Google Alerts, Talkwalker, F5Bot, and ForumScout
3. Scan native platform searches on X, Reddit, YouTube
4. Log anything notable into the tracking sheet
5. Flag items that need a response, correction, or amplification

This is not glamorous work. But the teams that do it consistently know where their brand stands in the citation layer, and the ones who skip it are guessing.
